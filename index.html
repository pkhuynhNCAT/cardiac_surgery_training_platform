📖 Comprehensive Introduction to the Mitral Valve

The mitral valve, also known as the bicuspid valve or left atrioventricular valve, represents one of the most complex and sophisticated structures in the human heart. This valve serves as the critical gateway between the left atrium and left ventricle, ensuring unidirectional blood flow and maintaining the efficiency of systemic circulation.

Historical Perspective:

The mitral valve derives its name from its resemblance to a bishop's mitre (a ceremonial headdress). First described in detail by Andreas Vesalius in 1543, the understanding of mitral valve anatomy has evolved significantly with advances in cardiac imaging and surgical techniques.

Anatomical Position and Orientation

The mitral valve is positioned in the left atrioventricular orifice, oriented approximately 45 degrees to the sagittal plane. The valve annulus lies in a plane that is tilted posteriorly and to the left, with the anterior portion higher than the posterior portion. This unique three-dimensional orientation is critical for understanding surgical approaches and imaging interpretation.

Mitral Valve Orifice Area (MVA) = 4.0 - 6.0 cm² (normal adult) Critical MVA for symptoms = < 1.5 cm² Severe stenosis = < 1.0 cm²

The Six Components of the Mitral Valve Complex

1. Mitral Annulus - The Foundation

The mitral annulus is a complex, dynamic, fibromuscular structure that serves as the insertion point for the valve leaflets. Unlike a simple ring, the annulus has a distinctive three-dimensional saddle shape that is crucial for optimal valve function.

  • Shape: D-shaped or kidney-bean configuration with distinct anterior and posterior portions
  • Composition: Fibrous tissue anteriorly (continuous with aortic valve), muscular tissue posteriorly
  • Dynamics: Changes size by 20-30% during cardiac cycle (systolic contraction)
  • Dimensions: Anteroposterior diameter: 32-38mm, Intercommissural distance: 35-40mm
  • Clinical significance: Annular dilatation is a primary mechanism of functional mitral regurgitation

2. Anterior Leaflet (Aortic Leaflet)

The anterior leaflet is the larger of the two leaflets in terms of surface area but occupies only one-third of the annular circumference. Its unique position and structure make it critical for valve competence.

  • Surface area: Approximately 60% of total leaflet area
  • Shape: Triangular or trapezoidal configuration
  • Height: 20-30mm from annulus to free edge
  • Thickness: 1-2mm at base, <1mm at free edge
  • Zones: Rough zone (coaptation area), Clear zone (central portion), Basal zone (attachment)
  • Fibrous continuity: Direct connection with left and non-coronary cusps of aortic valve

3. Posterior Leaflet (Mural Leaflet)

The posterior leaflet is structurally more complex with its characteristic scalloped appearance, occupying two-thirds of the annular circumference despite having less surface area than the anterior leaflet.

  • Scallops: Typically three - P1 (lateral/anterolateral), P2 (middle/central), P3 (medial/posteromedial)
  • Indentations: Natural clefts between scallops allow flexibility
  • Height: 10-15mm (shorter than anterior leaflet)
  • Width: Variable, P2 usually largest (45-60% of posterior leaflet)
  • Clinical relevance: P2 most commonly affected in degenerative disease

4. Chordae Tendineae - The Suspension System

The chordae tendineae are fibrous strings that connect the papillary muscles to the valve leaflets, preventing prolapse during systole. Their complex branching pattern and varying thickness serve different functional roles.

Two papillary muscles arise from the left ventricular wall and provide the attachment points for the chordae tendineae. Their synchronized contraction with the ventricle is essential for maintaining valve competence.

6. Left Ventricular Wall - The Foundation

The left ventricular wall provides the structural foundation for the papillary muscles and contributes to overall valve function through its geometry and contractility.

  • Geometric contribution: LV shape affects papillary muscle position and orientation
  • Contractile function: Synchronous contraction maintains optimal PM-annular relationship
  • Remodeling effects: LV dilatation causes PM displacement leading to functional MR
  • Ischemic changes: Regional wall motion abnormalities affect valve function

  3. Third-order: Multiple branches before insertion

🧬 Embryological Development of the Mitral Valve

Understanding the embryological development of the mitral valve is crucial for comprehending congenital abnormalities and provides insight into the complex anatomy of the mature valve.

Week 4-5: Endocardial Cushion Formation

The primitive heart tube undergoes looping, and endocardial cushions begin to form in the atrioventricular canal. These cushions are derived from endocardial cells that undergo epithelial-mesenchymal transformation (EMT).

  • Superior and inferior endocardial cushions fuse to divide AV canal
  • Lateral cushions contribute to valve formation
  • Neural crest cells contribute to cushion development

Week 6-7: Valve Leaflet Delamination

The valve leaflets begin to form through a process of delamination from the ventricular myocardium. This process is mediated by apoptosis and remodeling of the endocardial cushions.

  • Anterior leaflet derives from superior cushion
  • Posterior leaflet from inferior and lateral cushions
  • Commissures form at fusion points

Week 8-9: Chordae and Papillary Muscle Formation

Chordae tendineae and papillary muscles develop from the ventricular myocardium through a complex process of undermining and sculpting.

  • Myocardial tissue undergoes apoptosis except at attachment points
  • Remaining tissue forms papillary muscles and chordae
  • Chordae undergo fibrotic transformation

Week 10-12: Maturation and Refinement

The valve undergoes final maturation with development of the characteristic trilayer structure and establishment of proper dimensions.

  • Extracellular matrix organization
  • Collagen and elastin deposition
  • Establishment of leaflet zones
Clinical Correlations - Congenital Abnormalities:
  • Cleft mitral valve: Failure of cushion fusion (associated with AV canal defects)
  • Parachute mitral valve: Single papillary muscle due to developmental failure
  • Double orifice mitral valve: Abnormal fusion creating two openings
  • Mitral arcade: Failure of chordal development
  • Supravalvular mitral ring: Persistence of embryonic tissue

💓 Comprehensive Cardiac Cycle Physiology

Detailed Phases of Mitral Valve Function

Phase 1: Isovolumetric Relaxation (50-70ms)

Following aortic valve closure, the ventricle relaxes with all valves closed. LV pressure falls rapidly while volume remains constant.

  • LV pressure: Falls from ~120 to ~8 mmHg
  • LA pressure: ~8-10 mmHg (v wave peak)
  • Mitral valve: Closed, preparing to open
  • Calcium reuptake in sarcoplasmic reticulum

Phase 2: Early Rapid Filling (E Wave) (100ms)

When LV pressure falls below LA pressure, the mitral valve opens passively. 70-80% of ventricular filling occurs during this phase.

  • Pressure gradient: 4-8 mmHg (LA > LV)
  • Peak E velocity: 0.8-1.0 m/s (normal)
  • Deceleration time: 160-240ms
  • Ventricular suction effect contributes

Phase 3: Diastasis (Variable duration)

Period of minimal flow as LA and LV pressures equilibrate. Duration varies with heart rate.

  • Pressure gradient: <1 mmHg
  • Minimal transmitral flow
  • Disappears at high heart rates
  • Allows coronary perfusion

Phase 4: Atrial Systole (A Wave) (100ms)

Atrial contraction provides final 20-30% of ventricular filling. More important at high heart rates or in diastolic dysfunction.

  • Peak A velocity: 0.5-0.7 m/s (normal)
  • E/A ratio: 1.0-2.0 (age-dependent)
  • Atrial kick crucial in stiff ventricles
  • Lost in atrial fibrillation

Phase 5: Isovolumetric Contraction (50ms)

Rapid rise in LV pressure causes mitral valve closure. First heart sound (S1) produced.

  • LV pressure rises from ~10 to ~80 mmHg
  • Mitral valve closes at ~25 mmHg
  • Papillary muscles contract first
  • Annulus begins to contract

Phase 6: Systolic Ejection (250-300ms)

Mitral valve remains closed throughout systole, withstanding high pressure differential.

  • Peak LV pressure: 120-140 mmHg
  • LA pressure: 10-15 mmHg
  • Pressure differential: >100 mmHg
  • Annular area reduction: 25-30%
  • Papillary muscle shortening: 15-20%
Mitral Valve Area (Gorlin Formula): MVA = (CO/DFP) / (37.7 × √ΔP) Where: CO = Cardiac Output (mL/min) DFP = Diastolic Filling Period (sec/min) ΔP = Mean Pressure Gradient (mmHg)

Autonomic Innervation:
  • Parasympathetic (Vagus): Primarily affects AV node conduction
  • Sympathetic: Increases contractility of papillary muscles
  • Sensory fibers: Detect stretch and pressure changes
  • Intrinsic cardiac neurons: Local reflex circuits

🔬 Carpentier's Functional Classification - Complete Analysis

The Carpentier classification, introduced in 1983 and refined over decades, provides a functional framework for understanding mitral regurgitation based on leaflet motion rather than etiology. This system guides surgical decision-making and predicts repair techniques.

Type I: Normal Leaflet Motion

The leaflets move normally but fail to coapt properly due to annular dilatation or leaflet perforation.

Etiology Mechanism Echo Findings Surgical Approach
Annular dilatation Central jet from lack of coaptation Enlarged annulus, central MR jet Annuloplasty ring
Leaflet perforation Direct flow through defect Eccentric jet at perforation site Patch repair or resection
Cleft leaflet Congenital defect in leaflet Jet through cleft Cleft closure
Type II: Excessive Leaflet Motion (Prolapse)

The free edge of one or both leaflets overrides the plane of the annulus during systole.

Subtypes and Characteristics:

  • IIa - Chordal elongation: Leaflet billows above annular plane
  • IIb - Chordal rupture: Flail leaflet with eversion into atrium
  • IIc - Papillary muscle elongation: Entire apparatus displaced
Pathology Prevalence Age Group Repair Success
Fibroelastic deficiency 60-70% >60 years >95%
Barlow's disease 10-15% Young adults 85-90%
Forme fruste 20-25% Middle age >90%
Type III: Restricted Leaflet Motion

Leaflet motion is restricted, preventing proper closure.

Subclassification:

  • Type IIIa: Diastolic and systolic restriction (rheumatic, radiation)
  • Type IIIb: Systolic restriction only (functional/ischemic MR)

Mechanisms of Type IIIb (Functional MR)

1
LV dilatation: Increases interpapillary muscle distance
2
Papillary muscle displacement: Apical and lateral displacement
3
Tethering: Increased tethering forces on leaflets
4
Annular dilatation: Secondary annular enlargement
5
Reduced closing forces: Decreased LV contractility

Rheumatic Mitral Stenosis Progression

Stage 1: Acute Rheumatic Fever (0-6 weeks)

Acute inflammation with Aschoff bodies, verrucous vegetations along closure line. Usually subclinical valve involvement.

  • Pancarditis with valve edema
  • Mitral regurgitation more common than stenosis
  • Modified Jones criteria for diagnosis

Stage 2: Chronic Rheumatic Heart Disease (Years)

Progressive fibrosis, commissural fusion, and chordal thickening develop over 10-20 years.

  • Commissural fusion begins at anterolateral commissure
  • Leaflet thickening from tips toward base
  • Chordal thickening and fusion
  • Fish-mouth appearance develops

Stage 3: Symptomatic Stenosis (Variable onset)

Symptoms typically appear when MVA <2.5 cm². Progressive symptoms with further narrowing.

  • Exertional dyspnea (MVA <2.5 cm²)
  • Atrial fibrillation (MVA <1.5 cm²)
  • Pulmonary hypertension
  • Right heart failure

Stage 4: End-stage Disease

Severe calcification, fixed pulmonary hypertension, and multi-organ consequences.

  • Extensive calcification
  • Fixed pulmonary vascular resistance
  • Tricuspid regurgitation
  • Hepatic congestion

⚕️ Median Sternotomy - Gold Standard Approach

Step-by-Step Surgical Technique

1. Patient Preparation and Positioning

  • Supine position with roll under shoulders
  • Arms tucked at sides or on armboards <90°
  • Foley catheter, arterial line, central line placement
  • TEE probe insertion after intubation
  • Surgical timeout and antibiotic administration
  • Prep from chin to mid-thighs, table to table

2. Incision and Sternotomy

  • Skin incision from sternal notch to xiphoid
  • Electrocautery through subcutaneous tissue
  • Identify midline and mark with cautery
  • Oscillating saw for sternotomy (single pass preferred)
  • Bone wax for hemostasis
  • Sternal retractor placement (gradual opening)

3. Pericardiotomy and Cardiac Exposure

  • Inverted T pericardiotomy
  • Pericardial stay sutures (6-8 sutures)
  • Create pericardial cradle
  • Assess cardiac anatomy and adhesions
  • Harvest pericardium if needed (glutaraldehyde treatment)

4. Cannulation Strategy

  • Heparinization (300-400 units/kg, ACT >480s)
  • Aortic cannulation: Ascending aorta, 2 pursestrings
  • Venous cannulation options:
    • Bicaval: SVC and IVC with snares
    • Single two-stage: RA appendage
    • Femoral: Percutaneous for redo cases
  • LV vent via right superior pulmonary vein

5. Cardiopulmonary Bypass Initiation

  • Gradual initiation of bypass
  • Check venous drainage and arterial flow
  • Cool to 28-32°C (mild hypothermia)
  • Empty heart with venting
  • Aortic cross-clamp application
  • Cardioplegia delivery (antegrade ± retrograde)

Posterior Leaflet Procedures

Triangular Resection

Intermediate

Step-by-Step Technique

1
Identify prolapsing segment: Usually P2, confirm with nerve hook
2
Mark resection: Triangular wedge with 2:1 height:base ratio
3
Resect tissue: Include abnormal chords, preserve normal tissue
4
Annular plication: Figure-of-8 sutures if >1.5cm resection
5
Leaflet reconstruction: 5-0 Prolene, continuous or interrupted
6
Test competency: Saline injection, check for leaks
Pearls and Pitfalls:
  • Limit resection to <25% of posterior leaflet
  • Avoid excessive height reduction (maintain >8mm)
  • Consider sliding plasty for extensive prolapse
  • Ensure symmetric closure line

Sliding Plasty (Carpentier Technique)

Advanced

Indicated for extensive posterior leaflet prolapse (Barlow's disease) where simple resection would remove >25% of leaflet.

Technical Steps

1
Quadrangular resection: Remove prolapsing segment completely
2
Detach leaflet remnants: From annulus for 1-2cm on each side
3
Reduce height: Trim to 15mm if excessive
4
Slide leaflets: Advance segments together
5
Reattach to annulus: 4-0 Prolene, incorporate in ring sutures
6
Close leaflet: Continuous 5-0 Prolene

⚠️ Major Intraoperative Complications

Systolic Anterior Motion (SAM)

Pathophysiology and Risk Factors:
  • Mechanism: Anterior leaflet drawn into LVOT by Venturi effect
  • Risk factors:
    • Small LV cavity (EDD <45mm)
    • Excess posterior leaflet tissue
    • Small annuloplasty ring (<28mm)
    • Anterior leaflet: Annulus ratio >1.3
    • Narrow aorto-mitral angle (<120°)
    • Sigmoid septum

Management Algorithm

1
Medical Management (First Line):
  • Volume loading (500-1000ml)
  • Discontinue inotropes
  • Beta-blockade (esmolol/metoprolol)
  • Increase afterload (phenylephrine)
2
Surgical Revision (If Medical Fails):
  • Larger annuloplasty ring
  • Sliding posterior leaflet plasty
  • Anterior leaflet plication (Alfieri stitch)
  • Valve replacement (last resort)

Atrioventricular Disruption

Classification and Management:
Type Location Presentation Management
Type I Posterior AV groove Bleeding, hypotension Direct repair with pledgets
Type II Posterior annulus Massive bleeding Patch repair, consider bypass
Type III Complete disruption Exsanguination Emergency repair, ECMO standby
Prevention Strategies:
  • Gentle tissue handling, especially in elderly/calcified valves
  • Avoid excessive traction on leaflets
  • Careful debridement of calcium
  • Consider felt reinforcement in friable tissue
  • Maintain adequate exposure without excessive retraction

Circumflex Artery Injury

Risk Zones and Recognition:
  • Highest risk: P1 and anterolateral commissure (5-10mm from annulus)
  • Recognition: New inferior/lateral wall motion abnormalities, ST changes, arrhythmias
  • Prevention: Shallow bites (2-3mm), avoid deep sutures at P1
  • Management: Remove offending suture, consider CABG if persistent ischemia

Question 1 Anatomy

Which papillary muscle is more susceptible to ischemic dysfunction and why?

A) Anterolateral PM - single blood supply from LAD
B) Posteromedial PM - single blood supply from RCA or LCx
C) Both equally susceptible due to similar blood supply
D) Neither - papillary muscles have collateral circulation
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: B

The posteromedial papillary muscle is more susceptible to ischemic dysfunction because it typically has a single blood supply from either the RCA (dominant right system) or LCx (dominant left system). In contrast, the anterolateral papillary muscle has dual blood supply from both the LAD and LCx, providing collateral circulation that protects against ischemia. This anatomical difference explains why inferior MI more commonly causes papillary muscle rupture or dysfunction than anterior MI.

Question 2 Physiology

During which phase of the cardiac cycle does the mitral valve experience the greatest pressure differential, and what is the approximate magnitude?

A) Early diastole - 10-15 mmHg
B) Late diastole - 5-10 mmHg
C) Peak systole - 100-120 mmHg
D) Isovolumetric contraction - 50-60 mmHg
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: C

During peak systole, the mitral valve experiences the greatest pressure differential of approximately 100-120 mmHg. This occurs when LV pressure reaches its peak (120-140 mmHg) while LA pressure remains low (10-15 mmHg). This enormous pressure gradient explains why the mitral valve apparatus must be structurally robust, with strong chordae tendineae and properly functioning papillary muscles to prevent prolapse or regurgitation.

Question 3 Pathophysiology

According to Carpentier's classification, a patient with rheumatic mitral valve disease showing thickened, restricted leaflets with reduced mobility in both systole and diastole would be classified as:

A) Type I - Normal leaflet motion
B) Type II - Excessive leaflet motion
C) Type IIIa - Diastolic and systolic restriction
D) Type IIIb - Systolic restriction only
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: C

Type IIIa represents restriction in both diastole and systole, typically seen in rheumatic disease or radiation-induced valvulopathy. The leaflets are thickened and fibrotic with commissural fusion, limiting opening in diastole (causing stenosis) and closure in systole (causing regurgitation). Type IIIb, in contrast, shows only systolic restriction and is characteristic of functional/ischemic MR where tethering prevents proper systolic closure but diastolic opening is normal.

Question 4 Diagnosis

A patient with mitral stenosis has a mitral valve area of 0.8 cm² by planimetry and a mean gradient of 15 mmHg. The Wilkins score is 13 with extensive calcification. What is the most appropriate management?

A) Medical management with diuretics and beta-blockers
B) Percutaneous mitral balloon valvuloplasty
C) Surgical mitral valve replacement
D) Open mitral commissurotomy
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: C

This patient has severe mitral stenosis (MVA <1.0 cm²) with a high Wilkins score (13) indicating extensive calcification and poor valve morphology. A Wilkins score >12 predicts poor outcomes with balloon valvuloplasty (<50% success rate). The high mean gradient confirms severe stenosis requiring intervention. Given the unfavorable valve morphology, surgical mitral valve replacement is the most appropriate option. Open commissurotomy would also be unsuitable due to the extensive calcification.

Question 5 Surgical Technique

During mitral valve repair for P2 prolapse, you perform a triangular resection. What is the maximum percentage of the posterior leaflet that can be safely resected without compromising repair durability?

A) 10-15%
B) 20-25%
C) 30-35%
D) 40-50%
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: B

The maximum safe resection is 20-25% of the posterior leaflet. Beyond this, there's increased risk of creating excessive tension on the repair, leading to dehiscence or restricted leaflet motion. For more extensive prolapse requiring greater resection, a sliding plasty should be performed to redistribute tissue and reduce tension. This principle ensures adequate coaptation surface while maintaining leaflet mobility.

Question 6 Complications

A patient develops systolic anterior motion (SAM) after mitral repair with a posterior leaflet resection and 28mm annuloplasty ring. Initial TEE shows LVOT gradient of 50 mmHg. What is the FIRST appropriate intervention?

A) Immediate surgical revision with larger ring
B) Volume loading and discontinuation of inotropes
C) Initiation of dobutamine infusion
D) Placement of intra-aortic balloon pump
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: B

The first-line management for SAM is medical optimization. Volume loading increases LV cavity size, reducing the Venturi effect. Discontinuing inotropes decreases hyperdynamic LV function. Additional measures include beta-blockade to reduce contractility and phenylephrine to increase afterload. Only if medical management fails after adequate trial (including achieving euvolemia, beta-blockade, and vasoconstrictors) should surgical revision be considered. Dobutamine would worsen SAM by increasing contractility.

Question 7 Clinical Decision

A 72-year-old patient with severe symptomatic MR has the following findings: LVEF 35%, LVESD 52mm, LVEDD 68mm, PASP 65mmHg, Creatinine 2.1. What is the expected operative mortality risk and most appropriate management?

A) Low risk (<1%) - proceed with mitral repair
B) Intermediate risk (5-10%) - consider surgery after optimization
C) High risk (>15%) - consider transcatheter therapy
D) Prohibitive risk - medical management only
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: C

This patient has multiple high-risk features: LV dysfunction (EF 35%), severe LV dilatation (LVESD 52mm), pulmonary hypertension (PASP 65mmHg), and renal insufficiency (Cr 2.1). STS score would predict >15% operative mortality. Given the high surgical risk, transcatheter edge-to-edge repair (MitraClip) should be considered if anatomy is suitable. This aligns with current guidelines recommending TEER for symptomatic patients at high/prohibitive surgical risk.

Question 8 Anatomy

Which structure is at highest risk of injury when placing annuloplasty sutures at the posteromedial commissure?

A) Circumflex artery
B) Coronary sinus
C) AV node and Bundle of His
D) Left atrial appendage
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: C

The AV node is located at the apex of Koch's triangle, near the posteromedial commissure. The Bundle of His runs along the membranous septum just anterior to this area. Deep sutures in this region risk causing complete heart block. To avoid this complication, sutures should be placed more superficially and slightly away from the trigone area. The circumflex artery is at risk at the anterolateral commissure and P1 region, not the posteromedial commissure.

Question 9 Imaging

On TEE, the vena contracta width for mitral regurgitation is measured at 0.8 cm. According to ASE guidelines, this indicates:

A) Mild MR
B) Moderate MR
C) Severe MR
D) Cannot determine - need additional parameters
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: C

A vena contracta width ≥0.7 cm indicates severe MR according to ASE guidelines. The vena contracta is the narrowest portion of the regurgitant jet as it passes through the regurgitant orifice and is relatively independent of hemodynamic conditions. Values <0.3 cm indicate mild MR, while 0.3-0.69 cm suggests moderate MR. This measurement should be corroborated with other parameters like EROA, regurgitant volume, and pulmonary vein flow patterns.

Question 10 Pathophysiology

In functional mitral regurgitation due to inferior MI, which geometric alteration is the PRIMARY mechanism of regurgitation?

A) Annular dilatation
B) Papillary muscle rupture
C) Posterior-lateral papillary muscle displacement with leaflet tethering
D) Leaflet prolapse from elongated chordae
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: C

Inferior MI causes regional wall motion abnormality leading to posterior and lateral displacement of the posteromedial papillary muscle. This creates apical tethering of the mitral leaflets, preventing proper coaptation (Type IIIb - systolic restriction). While annular dilatation often coexists, it's a secondary phenomenon. Papillary muscle rupture is a rare acute complication, not the typical mechanism of functional MR. This understanding is crucial for surgical planning, as simple annuloplasty may not address the primary tethering problem.

Question 11 Surgical Planning

A 45-year-old patient with Barlow's disease has bileaflet prolapse with excess tissue. The anterior leaflet height is 35mm, posterior leaflet 20mm. What is the MOST important consideration to prevent SAM during repair?

A) Use the smallest possible annuloplasty ring
B) Perform aggressive posterior leaflet height reduction
C) Maintain anterior:posterior leaflet height ratio close to 2:1
D) Avoid any annuloplasty ring placement
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: C

Maintaining proper anterior:posterior leaflet height ratio (ideally 2:1) is crucial for preventing SAM. In Barlow's disease with excess tissue, the posterior leaflet should be reduced to approximately 15-17mm if the anterior leaflet is 35mm. Additionally, using a larger ring (32-36mm) rather than downsizing helps prevent SAM. The combination of proper leaflet height ratio and appropriate ring sizing minimizes the risk of creating the anatomic substrate for SAM.

Question 12 Guidelines

According to 2020 ACC/AHA guidelines, which scenario is a Class I indication for mitral valve surgery in asymptomatic severe primary MR?

A) LVEF 62%, LVESD 38mm
B) LVEF 58%, LVESD 39mm
C) LVEF 65%, LVESD 35mm with new atrial fibrillation
D) LVEF 70%, LVESD 32mm with PASP 45mmHg
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: B

Class I indications for surgery in asymptomatic severe primary MR include LVEF ≤60% OR LVESD ≥40mm. Option B with LVEF 58% meets the criteria for LV dysfunction. Options C and D (new AF, PASP >50mmHg) are Class IIa indications. The thresholds of LVEF ≤60% and LVESD ≥40mm were chosen based on studies showing worse postoperative outcomes when surgery is delayed beyond these points, even in asymptomatic patients.

Question 13 Transcatheter

Which anatomic criterion would make a patient UNSUITABLE for transcatheter edge-to-edge repair (MitraClip)?

A) Mitral valve area 3.5 cm²
B) Flail gap 8mm
C) Flail width 20mm
D) Mobile leaflet length 9mm
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: C

A flail width >15mm is considered unsuitable for MitraClip as it exceeds the device's grasping capability. The criteria for MitraClip suitability include: MVA >4.0 cm² (to avoid creating stenosis), flail gap <10mm, flail width <15mm, and mobile leaflet length >10mm for adequate tissue grasping. Option C with 20mm flail width exceeds the acceptable range and would likely result in procedural failure or inadequate MR reduction.

Question 14 Postoperative Care

A patient develops complete heart block 48 hours after mitral valve replacement. The most likely cause is:

A) Injury to the circumflex artery
B) Deep sutures near the anterolateral commissure
C) Edema/trauma near the AV node at the posteromedial commissure
D) Calcium embolization to the conduction system
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: C

Complete heart block after mitral surgery typically results from trauma or edema near the AV node, located at the posteromedial commissure (apex of Koch's triangle). The delayed onset (48 hours) suggests edema rather than direct injury. This may be temporary, so observation with temporary pacing for 7-10 days is appropriate before considering permanent pacemaker. Direct surgical injury would typically present immediately postoperatively.

Question 15 Hemodynamics

Calculate the mitral valve area using the pressure half-time method if PHT = 280 ms:

A) 0.6 cm²
B) 0.8 cm²
C) 1.0 cm²
D) 1.2 cm²
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: B

The formula for MVA using pressure half-time is: MVA = 220/PHT. Therefore, MVA = 220/280 = 0.79 cm², approximately 0.8 cm². This indicates severe mitral stenosis (MVA <1.0 cm²). PHT >220 ms generally indicates severe MS. This method can be inaccurate in conditions affecting LA or LV compliance, immediately post-valvuloplasty, or with significant aortic regurgitation.

Question 16 Surgical Technique

When performing artificial chordae implantation with Gore-Tex, what is the recommended technique for determining optimal chord length?

A) Make it equal to adjacent normal chordae
B) Use Carpentier calipers set to annular plane
C) Fill ventricle with saline and adjust to coaptation point
D) Use pre-measured loops based on valve size
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: C

The most reliable method is filling the ventricle with saline and adjusting the chord length until proper leaflet position is achieved at the coaptation point. This "saline test" method allows real-time assessment of leaflet height and coaptation. While reference to adjacent chordae and calipers can help, the saline test provides functional confirmation. Pre-measured loops are convenient but may not account for individual variation.

Question 17 Complications

A patient presents 6 weeks post mitral valve repair with fever, new MR, and positive blood cultures for Streptococcus viridans. What is the most appropriate management?

A) 6 weeks of IV antibiotics alone
B) 2 weeks of IV antibiotics then reassess
C) Immediate surgery plus antibiotics
D) Complete antibiotic course then elective surgery
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: C

This represents early prosthetic valve endocarditis (PVE) with repair failure (new MR), which is a Class I indication for urgent surgery. Early PVE (<12 months post-op) has high mortality with medical therapy alone and often involves prosthetic material infection. S. viridans, while typically less virulent, requires surgery in the setting of early PVE with hemodynamic compromise. Surgery should include complete debridement, ring removal, and re-repair or replacement.

Question 18 Imaging

Which TEE view best visualizes the P2 segment of the posterior mitral leaflet?

A) Mid-esophageal 4-chamber at 0°
B) Mid-esophageal commissural at 60°
C) Mid-esophageal long-axis at 120°
D) Transgastric basal short-axis at 0°
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: C

The mid-esophageal long-axis view at 120-140° (also called 2-chamber view) best visualizes P2 and opposing A2 segments. This view cuts through the middle of the mitral valve, showing the most common site of degenerative prolapse. The commissural view (60°) shows P1/A1 and P3/A3, while the 4-chamber view (0°) provides lateral segments. Question 19 Research

According to the COAPT trial, which patients with functional MR benefited most from transcatheter edge-to-edge repair?

A) Severe LV dilatation (LVEDV >250ml) with severe MR
B) Moderate LV dilatation with disproportionately severe MR
C) Any degree of LV dysfunction with moderate MR
D) Preserved LV function with exercise-induced MR
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: B

COAPT demonstrated benefit in patients with moderate LV dilatation but disproportionately severe MR (EROA ≥0.3 cm²), suggesting the MR was driving heart failure rather than just being a consequence. Patients with severe LV dilatation (>250ml) - "proportionate MR" as in MITRA-FR - showed less benefit, likely because MR was a marker of advanced cardiomyopathy rather than the primary problem. This concept of "proportionate vs disproportionate MR" helps identify optimal TEER candidates.

Question 20 Anatomy

The mitral annulus undergoes what percentage of area reduction during normal systolic contraction?

A) 5-10%
B) 15-20%
C) 25-30%
D) 35-40%
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: C

The normal mitral annulus contracts by 25-30% during systole, contributing significantly to valve competence. This dynamic motion is most pronounced in the posterior annulus (muscular portion) while the anterior annulus (fibrous portion) remains relatively fixed. This systolic contraction reduces annular area, facilitating leaflet coaptation. Loss of annular contraction in AF or with rigid annuloplasty rings may contribute to functional MR.

Question 21 Pathophysiology

What is the most common cause of mitral valve prolapse in developed countries?

A) Rheumatic heart disease
B) Myxomatous degeneration
C) Ischemic heart disease
D) Infective endocarditis
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: B

Myxomatous degeneration is the most common cause of mitral valve prolapse in developed countries, affecting 2-3% of the population. It's characterized by accumulation of mucopolysaccharides in the spongiosa layer, leading to leaflet thickening, redundancy, and chordal elongation. This can range from mild billowing to severe Barlow's disease. Rheumatic disease causes restriction, not prolapse. Ischemic disease causes tethering (restriction), not prolapse.

Question 22 Surgical Decision

A 78-year-old patient needs mitral valve surgery. Which factor most strongly favors replacement over repair?

A) Isolated P2 prolapse
B) Anterior leaflet rheumatic changes with calcification
C) Annular dilatation without leaflet disease
D) Need for concomitant CABG
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: B

Rheumatic disease with anterior leaflet calcification is a strong indication for replacement. Repair of calcified rheumatic valves has poor durability with high reoperation rates. In elderly patients, a durable single operation (replacement) is often preferable to risking early repair failure. Isolated P2 prolapse is the ideal repair lesion. Age alone doesn't contraindicate repair, and concomitant CABG doesn't influence repair vs replacement decision.

Question 23 Anticoagulation

A patient with a mechanical mitral valve on warfarin (INR target 2.5-3.5) needs urgent surgery. The INR is 3.2. What is the appropriate management?

A) Proceed with surgery immediately
B) Give vitamin K 10mg IV and wait 24 hours
C) Give 4-factor PCC and proceed when INR <1.5
D) Hold warfarin for 5 days then proceed
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: C

For urgent surgery in anticoagulated patients, 4-factor prothrombin complex concentrate (PCC) provides rapid reversal within 30 minutes. Low-dose vitamin K (1-2mg) can be given concurrently to sustain reversal. FFP is less effective and requires large volumes. Waiting 5 days isn't appropriate for urgent surgery. After surgery, bridging with heparin is needed until therapeutic INR is re-achieved to minimize thrombotic risk with the mechanical valve.

Question 24 Minimally Invasive

What is the main advantage of robotic mitral valve surgery compared to conventional mini-thoracotomy?

A) Shorter cross-clamp times
B) Lower mortality rates
C) Enhanced 3D visualization and instrument articulation
D) Ability to perform more complex repairs
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: C

The main advantage of robotic surgery is enhanced 3D high-definition visualization and wristed instrument articulation allowing precise movements in confined spaces. This improves ergonomics for the surgeon. However, robotic procedures typically have longer cross-clamp times due to setup complexity. Mortality rates and repair complexity capabilities are similar between robotic and mini-thoracotomy approaches. The choice depends on surgeon experience and patient factors.

Question 25 Congenital

A 6-month-old infant has a mitral valve with a single papillary muscle and restricted leaflet motion. This describes:

A) Mitral valve prolapse
B) Parachute mitral valve
C) Double orifice mitral valve
D) Cleft mitral valve
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: B

Parachute mitral valve is characterized by insertion of all chordae into a single papillary muscle, creating a funnel-shaped valve with restricted mobility. Question 26 Emergency

A patient develops acute severe MR 5 days post-MI with cardiogenic shock. Echo shows flail posterior leaflet. What is the most likely diagnosis and management?

A) Functional MR - medical management with inotropes
B) Papillary muscle rupture - emergency surgery
C) LV remodeling - IABP and delayed surgery
D) Annular dilatation - diuresis and ACE inhibitors
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: B

Papillary muscle rupture is a mechanical complication occurring 2-7 days post-MI, presenting with acute severe MR and cardiogenic shock. The posteromedial papillary muscle is more commonly affected (6:1 ratio) due to single blood supply. Echo shows flail leaflet with hypermobile papillary muscle head. This is a surgical emergency with 75% mortality without surgery. IABP can provide temporary stabilization, but immediate surgical repair or replacement is required for survival.

Question 27 Prosthetic Valves

What is the expected gradient across a normally functioning 29mm bioprosthetic mitral valve?

A) 1-2 mmHg
B) 3-5 mmHg
C) 8-10 mmHg
D) 12-15 mmHg
📚 Detailed Explanation

Correct Answer: B

Normal mean gradients for bioprosthetic mitral valves are 3-5 mmHg for sizes ≥27mm. Smaller valves (25mm) may have gradients of 5-7 mmHg. Question 29 Echocardiography

The coaptation length of mitral leaflets after successful repair should be at least:

A) 2-3 mm
B) 5-8 mm
C) 10-12 mm
D) 15-20 mm
📚 Detailed Explanation
